GIGO: The Root of AI Bias

Artificial Intelligence

Artificial Intelligence, otherwise known as AI, is seen as objective - a machine without emotion or prejudice, which plays a role in many daily functions ranging from simple tasks such as creating dinner menus or delicious recipes that take the shortest amount of time to cook, using old stale freezer burned chicken as the key ingredient; to more business oriented tasks such as résumé screening and helping Human Resources teams decide who gets called for a job interview or who gets hired, credit scoring or loan eligibility and approvals, etc. Other specialised tasks which AI now influences include patient care in how a doctor assesses a patient, in government and public administration, in agriculture or even in carrying out more systematic tasks such as in aerospace, for example, Ingenuity (Ginny, for short), NASA’s AI-powered Mars Helicopter, which made history by successfully flying on Mars, demonstrating the potential for autonomous navigation and exploration in challenging environments. https://www.aerostudent.org/article/how-ingenuity-proved-flight-is-possible-on-mars

Feeding the Machine Garbage

AI systems learn by studying examples and drawing information from patterns shown in whatever data input it is provided with. If those examples are fair, the AI can learn to make fair decisions. AI is not human and does not understand context, so if the data provided is bad, incomplete, mislabeled, flawed, biased or negatively skewed; then the AI system will carry forward those flaws it learned from the distorted input and such flawed information can be dressed up to sound credible. This is how AI bias appears in real life: not because the AI itself is “bad,” but because it learned from information that was incomplete from the start. In some cases, the AI confidently produces convincing outputs which reflect the systems, social structures and low-quality data the model was built from, leading to harmful and poor-quality outcomes. This is called the GIGO principle. GIGO means “Garbage In, Garbage Out”, where unbalanced inputs result in flawed and unreliable outputs. GIGO is a term I learned in the Y2K era of early computing. It shows that computers produce exactly what they received. In modern day terms, GIGO can be termed as AI bias, which refers to unfair outcomes produced specifically by machine learning or AI systems. AI bias occurs when bad, poor-quality data used to train a model, leads to system distortions and causes the model to produce unfair outputs, because like computers, AI is as good as the data it is built on. If the data has discrimination, skewed information, stereotypes, or other false assumptions, then the AI system will operate on that distorted reality. An example of such AI bias is observed when AI hiring tools, models and résumé screening systems favour certain applicants over others due to skewed historical/past hiring data, such as tools rejecting resumes from women more than that from men. The failure is due to the skewed, discriminatory and biased historical data used in training the model.

AI Bias

AI bias is the behaviour that results from GIGO and is one of the most common harmful forms of “garbage out,” because biased data leads to AI models reproducing or even amplifying those biases. AI bias is not a glitch. It is a mirror reflecting the data, systems, and social structures it was built from. GIGO explains why it happens. Algorithmic bias explains how it happens. Fairness in AI explains what we can do to fix it. Examples of GIGO resulting in AI bias include:

• A facial recognition model misidentifying darker‑skinned faces because its training data lacked diversity and led to multiple wrongful arrests.

• Incomplete training data reflecting historical inequalities for underrepresented medical groups resulting in diagnostic tools performing worse for certain demographic groups.

Algorithmic Bias

Algorithmic bias describes systematic and repeated unfairness caused by biased data, design choices, or evaluation processes. It occurs when an algorithm repeatedly produces unfair decisions because the system is reflecting what it learned or the rules it was given when it was designed - bias that comes from the data, the design, or the way the system is used. Algorithmic bias existed long before the onset of AI. An example of algorithmic bias (before the existence of AI), is observed in credit scoring scenarios where discriminatory lending decisions were made because race, income and historical segregation was used to penalize people living in certain postal codes, so they have lower approval rates, even if they have better financial profiles than people in higher income neighbourhoods. Understanding algorithmic bias is fundamental for building fair and trustworthy AI.

Reducing AI Bias and Improving AI Fairness

Fairness in AI matters because AI tools affect everyday life. It impacts jobs, healthcare, credit, policing, etc; and unfair systems can cause real harm such as misjudging risk for certain groups because the training data did not include enough diverse patients as seen in the case of healthcare risk models and medical algorithms underestimating Black patients’ illnesses and needs due to the models using healthcare spending as a proxy for health. But historically, Black patients receive less health care, so they appeared “healthier” in the data.

Strategies for promoting fairness in AI include:

• Improve data quality by collecting more diverse and representative data, including all demographic groups, especially underrepresented ones to ensure data integrity, accuracy, completeness, and consistency

• Before training an AI system, audit datasets for bias and use human in the loop checks for high risk decisions

• Continuously and regularly update and monitor models as data changes and check for bias regularly

• Clearly document data sources and limitations

• Improve labeling and review processes

Other fairness techniques include:

• Pre processing: data cleaning, fixing or rebalancing

• In processing: building fairness into models and modifying algorithms to reduce bias

• Post processing: adjusting models and outputs when problems appear to meet fairness criteria

• Governance: ongoing review and monitoring, data audits and human oversight by keeping humans involved in important decisions to ensure equitable treatment, transparency and accountability

The solution to AI bias starts with input of better data - more complete, more representative, and more carefully reviewed data. Fairness in AI is not a one time task, but an ongoing responsibility. It starts early at the onset with input of high-quality data, in the design of AI system through to governance and continuously data review and monitoring. If we want fair AI, we must start with fair information, and this is entirely within our control. By improving the data, strengthening the systems around it, checking for bias, and keeping humans involved; AI systems will learn to treat people more fairly and reflect human values.
