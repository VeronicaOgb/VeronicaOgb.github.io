Anonymous and Tracked!

The Login Hoax

At the completion of a course, a survey is set up, and students must access a URL in their web browser, log in with their username and password to provide responses for course evaluation. Responses are said to be treated as anonymous. A paradox, as students are promised anonymity after completing a login process which makes them identifiable. The login requirement works against data privacy and transparency causing informed consent failure due to misleading information. This failure results in students’ mistrust of the survey process and data privacy irregularities because they do not understand why their identity or login details are needed from the onset of the survey. Authenticated anonymity weakens the claim of anonymity and undermines several GDPR principles. The core idea of the GDPR principle is to collect less, explain more, and protect everything. In practice, this means collect only the data that is necessary, including any information that can identify an individual; explain clearly what data will be collected, why it is needed, and how it will be used; and protect or safeguard any data that is retained, stored, or processed.

The Data Logs

Later in the survey, the course name of the course is shown, while the student’s name is not. Even if the system does not store student names, the login requirement at the start makes it possible for the respondents to be traced easily since the system must authenticate the respondent by collecting and logging details such as IP address, login time, account ID, etc. Also, users’ login details make them traceable as the system can link responses to identities. There is also the risk of re-identification because even without login data, the course name itself allows the individual students to be identifiable in cases where the class has only a few students taking the course, or if the course is taken by a unique group of students such as people in specific roles, or free‑text responses such as unique word choices, recurring syntax errors, references to specific assignment grades, or even habitual use of “all-lowercase” text make an individual instantly recognizable, thereby revealing clues about the identity of the respondent.

Data Minimization

Data minimization is a core principle in data ethics framework, where only the data needed for a task is collected, and nothing more than the amount of data required is collected for the achievement of that specific task. The need for login requirements cannot be justified because a truly anonymous survey should not need any login details. The survey description indicates that all answers provided will remain anonymous, but this is false anonymity because the students must first authenticate who they are. Ethical tension is obvious where students feel pressured to respond as they may believe that the survey completion and their responses will be monitored or tied to their final grade or performance rating in the course. Students may feel coerced and fear negative consequences due to their honest feedback, so student participation is not voluntary, and fairness is ruled out because the responses carry a risk of grading retaliation from professors, thereby shifting from honest critique to polite compliance. The survey purpose also indirectly raises concerns about accountability because executive management wishes to use the results of the survey to determine which courses require a redesign or an update. The school must show responsible stewardship, by enforcing strict access controls and ensuring that identity data is not misused. The first question on the survey being “this course meets all of the course objectives” does not state what objective or data,executive management seeks to collate as the question is quite ambiguous. Survey questions should be defined and have a stated outcome to be achieved. Unstructured and non-specific questions are not aligned with expectations. Again, the transparency requirement comes in as the survey should clearly explain what data is being collected, why it is needed, and how it will be used. This clarity improves visibility, supports understanding, and helps build trust in the survey process.

Recommendations to Management

A data analyst assigned to this task may suggest certain better ways to ensure that the school or department adopts global best practices and ethical obligations. These suggestions may include the following recommendations:

Removing unnecessary login requirements: The data analyst should first address the issue created by mandatory login requirements. Students should not be required to log in with identifiable credentials to complete an anonymous survey, as this may conflict with data ethics principles and applicable AI regulations. If student confirmation is a requirement, then one-time access codes or tokens instead of login with username and password can be used for authentication purposes.

Providing a clear privacy notice: The informed consent issue can be addressed by publishing a brief notice that explains the survey’s data privacy conditions regarding:

• why access control is required;

• how anonymity is protected and how identity is separated from survey responses;

• how long data logs are retained or how quickly they are deleted; and

• who can access these logs, including whether administrators can view information that may indirectly identify survey participants.

Escalating the inconsistencies: The data analyst should inform the leadership team about the survey design not providing full anonymity and explain the design limitations and the conflict between the survey’s anonymity claim and the login process, which may allow respondents to be traced. Raising the issue ensures that management does not mistakenly treat the survey data as fully anonymous. This way, accidental data ethics violations are prevented in the long run.

If Management Rejects the Recommendations: In situations where management does not accept the proposed changes recommended by the data analyst, the data analyst should document the identified risks, clarify the limitations of the survey design and assess the possible implications. A plan should also be prepared to reduce the harm these violations could cause. The harm - reduction plan may involve ensuring the following:

• increasing aggregation thresholds for small groups;

• withholding the production of reports for group sizes with fewer than five respondents;

• avoiding any link between survey responses and login data and;

• preventing the production of reports that could indirectly identify individual students.
